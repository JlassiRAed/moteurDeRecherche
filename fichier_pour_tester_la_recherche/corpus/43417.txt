 D sharp minor is a minor scale based on D Its key signature has six sharps E flat minor is its enharmonic equivalent Its relative key relative major is F sharp major and its parallel key parallel major is D sharp major This is usually replaced by E flat major because D sharp major s two double sharps make it impractical to use Since D sharp minor and its enharmonic equivalent both have key signatures of six accidental s neither one is clearly used instead of the other For example using the melodic minor version of D sharp minor on the harp is very difficult The B pedal can be set to the sharp position but there is no double sharp position to set the C pedal at In Book I of The Well Tempered Clavier Bach chose to write the eighth prelude in E flat minor but the accompanying fugue in D sharp minor Music written in this key is considered very difficult to read which means that little music was written mainly in this key in the Classical era E flat minor is easier for many brass instruments and woodwinds The most famous work in this key is Scriabin s famous Etude Op No Etude Op No Russian composer Sergei Lyapunov Lyapunov must also have liked the key because he wrote the second etude of his Op set in the key and also fifteen years later his Variations on a Russian Theme Op His early Piano Concerto No Op was also in the enharmonic key of E flat minor In a few scores sharp key signatures in the bass clef are written with the sharp for the A on the top line This is not done often because it is different from the treble clef This key is not used often in orchestral music but is used more in keyboard music When arranging this piano music for orchestra some people recommend transposing the music to D minor or E minor If D sharp minor must be used B flat wind instruments should have their parts written in F minor rather than E sharp minor
